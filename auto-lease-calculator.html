What This Auto Lease Calculator Does

This calculator computes your estimated monthly lease payment for a vehicle, breaking down the key components: depreciation fee, finance fee, and taxes. Unlike buying, where you finance the full vehicle price, leasing means financing only the vehicle's depreciation during the lease term — plus interest (the money factor) and fees. Understanding the math behind a lease quote helps you negotiate more effectively and recognize when dealer numbers are inflated.

How It Works

A lease payment has three components:

Depreciation Fee = (Capitalized Cost − Residual Value) ÷ Lease Term (months)

Finance Fee = (Capitalized Cost + Residual Value) × Money Factor

Monthly Payment = Depreciation Fee + Finance Fee + Taxes

The capitalized cost is the negotiated vehicle price minus down payment or cap cost reductions. The residual value is what the manufacturer says the car will be worth at lease end (as a percentage of MSRP). The money factor is the interest rate in disguise — multiply by 2,400 to convert to approximate APR.

Key Concepts Explained

Money Factor

Money factor (or lease rate) is the financing cost expressed as a tiny decimal, typically 0.00100–0.00300. A money factor of 0.00200 equals approximately 4.8% APR. Dealers sometimes mark up the money factor above the manufacturer's base rate — ask for the "buy rate" and compare to published rates on sites like Edmunds.

Residual Value

Set by the manufacturer's financial arm and non-negotiable. Higher residuals mean lower monthly payments because you're financing less depreciation. Vehicles with high residuals — often luxury brands or EVs with incentives — are typically the best lease candidates.

Mileage Allowance

Leases typically allow 10,000, 12,000, or 15,000 miles per year. Exceeding this costs $0.15–$0.30 per mile at lease end. If you drive more than average, negotiate higher mileage at signing where extra miles are cheaper than paying overage fees at return.

Tips & Best Practices

  • Negotiate the vehicle price first, then discuss leasing — the cap cost is negotiable just like a purchase price. Focus on cap cost, not monthly payment.
  • Check manufacturer incentives — automakers frequently subsidize leases with inflated residuals or lowered money factors on specific models.
  • Avoid leasing if you drive heavily — if you drive 18,000+ miles annually, excess mileage fees almost always make leasing more expensive than buying.
  • Don't put money down on a lease — a down payment reduces monthly payments but provides no financial benefit; if the car is totaled, you lose that money.
  • Read the disposition fee — most leases charge $300–$500 when you return the car and don't lease or buy another from the same brand.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it better to lease or buy a car?

Leasing is better if you prefer lower monthly payments, want a new car every 2–3 years, drive fewer than 15,000 miles per year, and use the vehicle for business (lease payments may be deductible). Buying is better if you drive heavily, plan to keep the car long-term, or want to build equity. Over a 10-year period, owning consistently outperforms leasing in total cost.

Can I negotiate a lease deal?

Yes — the capitalized cost, acquisition fee, and dealer-added accessories are negotiable. Residual value and base money factor set by the manufacturer are not. Get competing quotes from multiple dealers on the same trim and use them as leverage. Never reveal you want to lease until you've agreed on the vehicle price.

What happens if I want to exit my lease early?

Early termination is expensive — you typically owe all remaining payments plus a termination fee minus current market value. Alternatives include transferring the lease to another person via Swapalease.com, buying out the lease if the residual is below market, or rolling the balance into a new lease with the same manufacturer.

Is gap coverage included in leases?

Most manufacturer leases include gap protection (covering the difference between the car's value and what you owe if totaled), but verify this before signing. Third-party leases arranged through a dealership financing company may not include gap coverage automatically.
