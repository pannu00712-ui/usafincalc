Data Sources

Every number on USAFinCalc comes from an official, primary U.S. government source — never a third-party estimate or another site's table. Here's exactly where each figure comes from, what it feeds into, and how to verify it yourself.

📅 Update schedule, in short: Federal and state tax figures are reviewed every fall and January following official releases. Rate-sensitive data is checked more often. Every change ships with a visible revision date. Full schedule below.

Primary Sources

These five agencies supply the figures behind nearly every calculator on the site.

IRS
Internal Revenue Service
Federal income tax brackets, standard deductions, filing-status rules, Additional Medicare Tax thresholds, and annual retirement contribution limits — sourced from IRS revenue procedures and Publication 15 (Circular E).
→ irs.gov — Tax Inflation Adjustments
Used in: Federal Tax Calculator, Salary Calculator, 401(k) Calculator, Capital Gains Tax
SSA
Social Security Administration
The annual Social Security wage base, employee FICA rate, and Medicare rate, plus benefit and full-retirement-age rules used in retirement withdrawal planning. Updated each October for the following year.
→ ssa.gov — Contribution & Benefit Base
Used in: Salary Calculator, Federal Tax Calculator, Retirement Withdrawal Calculator
STATE
State Tax Agencies (all 50 states)
State income tax brackets, standard deductions, and sales tax rates, pulled directly from each state's Department of Revenue (or equivalent) publications. Cross-checked against the Tax Foundation's independent state tax tracker before publishing.
→ taxadmin.org — Directory of State Tax Agencies
Used in: State Tax Calculators (all 50 states), State Salary-After-Tax pages
BLS
Bureau of Labor Statistics
Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS), Consumer Price Index (CPI) data for inflation adjustments, and Consumer Expenditure Survey figures used in cost-of-living comparisons.
→ bls.gov — Occupational Employment Statistics
Used in: Salary Calculator, Cost of Living Calculator, Hourly-to-Salary Calculator
FED
Federal Reserve (FRED — St. Louis Fed)
Historical S&P 500 returns, average 30-year fixed mortgage rates, the federal funds rate, and inflation series (CPI and PCE) used as defaults and reference ranges in rate-based calculators.
→ fred.stlouisfed.org
Used in: Mortgage Calculator, Refinance Calculator, Compound Interest Calculator, Inflation Calculator

How We Verify Each Figure

A number doesn't reach a calculator just because it sounds right. It follows the same sequence every time:

  1. Pull it from the primary document

    Every figure starts at the source — an IRS revenue procedure, an SSA bulletin, a state Department of Revenue notice, a BLS release, or a FRED data series. We record the specific publication, not a paraphrase of it.

  2. Build the formula around it

    The figure goes into the calculator's logic, then gets tested against a worked example from the same agency — an IRS worksheet, for instance — to confirm the output matches exactly.

  3. Cross-check where possible

    State-level numbers get a second, independent check against the Tax Foundation's tracking before anything ships, to catch a transcription error or an outdated source on our end.

  4. Publish with a visible date

    Every trust page and calculator carries the date it was last reviewed, so it's clear whether a figure reflects the current tax year.

This process is maintained by USAFinCalc's research team. See the editorial policy for the standards behind it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where does USAFinCalc get its tax and financial data?

Directly from primary government sources — the IRS, Social Security Administration, individual state tax agencies, the Bureau of Labor Statistics, and the Federal Reserve. We don't use third-party aggregator data for anything that affects a calculator's output.

How often is the data updated?

Federal tax, SSA, and state figures are reviewed every fall and January after official annual releases. Rate-sensitive data like mortgage rates is refreshed more often. Every update carries a visible revision date.

How do you verify a number before publishing it?

Each figure is sourced from the original government document, built into the calculator, then tested against a worked example from the same agency. State-level figures get a second cross-check against an independent tracker like the Tax Foundation before publishing.
