About New Jersey income tax

New Jersey levies a state income tax of approximately 6.37% on wage income. This is applied alongside federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare withholdings.

For most workers earning $50,000–$200,000 in New Jersey, the effective state tax rate is typically a few percentage points below the nominal rate after standard deductions and credits are applied. Use the calculator above for your exact figure.

What This New Jersey Income Tax Calculator Does

This calculator computes your New Jersey state income tax liability based on your gross income, filing status, and applicable deductions. It shows your estimated state tax owed, your effective New Jersey tax rate, and your after-tax take-home income — separate from federal taxes.

It's useful for anyone who earns income in New Jersey: W-2 employees checking that their withholding is accurate, self-employed workers estimating quarterly payments, people considering relocating to or from New Jersey and evaluating the tax impact, and anyone doing year-end tax planning who wants to estimate their New Jersey liability before filing.

State income tax is often underestimated as a component of total tax burden. For a household earning $100,000 in New Jersey, state income tax alone can represent a meaningful portion of take-home pay — and it interacts with federal taxes in ways that affect your overall effective rate.

Understanding Your Results

New Jersey Tax Owed

This is your estimated New Jersey state income tax liability after standard deductions and exemptions. It's what you'd owe on a New Jersey state return. If your employer withholds state tax from each paycheck, the result here tells you whether you're on track for a refund, a balance due, or roughly even.

Effective State Tax Rate

Your total state tax divided by your gross income — expressed as a percentage. This is the more useful number for comparing New Jersey's tax burden to other states, since marginal rates (the rate on the last dollar earned) can be misleading when comparing graduated and flat-rate states.

After-Tax Income (State)

Your gross income minus New Jersey state tax. Note that this doesn't account for federal income tax, Social Security, or Medicare — those are separate. Your true take-home also requires subtracting federal tax obligations.

New Jersey Income Tax Overview

New Jersey has one of the highest top marginal income tax rates in the country at 10.75%, applied to income above $1 million. Combined with a 2.5% surcharge on income over $5 million added in recent years, high earners in NJ face a significant state tax burden.

Current Tax Rate: 1.4%–10.75%

Deductions

New Jersey does not follow federal deduction rules — it has its own limited deduction structure and does not allow a standard deduction equivalent. Allowable deductions include medical expenses, alimony, and certain business expenses.

Key Credits

NJ Earned Income Tax Credit (40% of federal credit), property tax deduction/credit for homeowners and renters, dependent care credit, and pension exclusions for retirees.

Local Taxes

Some cities and municipalities in New Jersey may assess additional local income or wage taxes. These are not reflected in this state-level calculator. Check with your local government if you live or work in a municipality that may have additional local tax obligations.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Single Filer, $55,000 Salary

A single New Jersey resident earning $55,000 in wages applies the standard deduction and personal exemption. The resulting taxable income is then taxed at the applicable New Jersey rate(s). The effective New Jersey rate in this range is typically lower than the marginal rate due to deductions reducing the taxable base.

Example 2: Married Couple, $130,000 Combined Income

Married filers in New Jersey typically benefit from a higher standard deduction. On $130,000 combined income, the couple applies the married filing jointly deduction first, reducing their taxable income before applying the state rate(s). The result illustrates why married couples in many states face a somewhat lower effective rate than two single filers at the same income.

Example 3: Self-Employed Individual, $80,000 Net Profit

A freelancer or contractor in New Jersey with $80,000 in net business income owes state income tax on that income. The calculator helps estimate how much to set aside each quarter.

A high-income earner moving from California (13.3% top rate) to New Jersey (1.4%–10.75%) can run both calculations to see the annual state tax savings. Common Mistakes

Confusing marginal rate with effective rate

The top bracket rate gets the most attention, but your effective New Jersey tax rate — total tax divided by total income — is usually significantly lower, especially at middle incomes. Use effective rate when comparing states or estimating your actual burden.

Ignoring withholding adjustments after a major income change

A raise, bonus, second job, or new rental income can change your New Jersey tax liability meaningfully. If your employer's withholding doesn't update to match, you may owe a balance at filing time — plus potential underpayment penalties.

Not accounting for New Jersey-specific deductions or credits

Many residents miss credits and deductions unique to New Jersey — earned income credits, dependent care credits, property tax credits — that reduce actual liability below what a generic calculation shows. Review the credits listed above before finalizing your estimate.

Part-year residents filing as full-year residents

If you moved to or from New Jersey during the tax year, you file as a part-year resident and are only taxed on income earned while you were a New Jersey resident. Filing as a full-year resident when you weren't would result in overpaying state taxes.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Check your withholding once a year — especially after a raise, job change, or significant life event. Your New Jersey W-4 equivalent tells your employer how much state tax to withhold.
  • If self-employed, set aside New Jersey estimated tax quarterly — the due dates align closely with federal quarterly dates (April, June, September, January).
  • Run this calculator in October or November to check where you stand before year-end — you still have time to adjust withholding or make a final estimated payment.
  • Consider the full state tax picture when comparing New Jersey to other states — property tax, sales tax, and other levies matter alongside income tax rates.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does New Jersey income tax interact with federal taxes?

They're calculated separately. Your federal return uses your federal deductions and brackets; your New Jersey return uses state-specific rules. State income taxes you pay may be deductible on your federal return if you itemize (subject to the $10,000 SALT cap).

Does New Jersey tax Social Security income?

Yes, although lower-income retirees may qualify for exclusions or credits. Check current New Jersey Department of Revenue guidance for the current year thresholds.

What if I work in New Jersey but live in another state?

You'd typically owe income tax in both states — your resident state taxes your worldwide income, while New Jersey taxes income earned within its borders. Your home state usually offers a credit for taxes paid to other states to prevent full double taxation.

How do I pay New Jersey estimated taxes if I'm self-employed?

Most states use a quarterly estimated payment system similar to the federal system. Visit the New Jersey Department of Revenue website for payment portal access. Payments are generally due in April, June, September, and January.

Are capital gains taxed differently in New Jersey?

In most states, capital gains are taxed as ordinary income at the same rates as wages. New Jersey follows this general rule — check current state guidance for any special treatment of long-term capital gains.

When is the New Jersey state tax return due?

Most states align their filing deadline with the federal April 15 deadline. New Jersey generally follows this schedule, with the same extension provisions — filing for a federal extension typically also extends your New Jersey deadline, though any taxes owed are still due by the original deadline.

Key Takeaways

Your New Jersey state income tax is one piece of your total tax picture — but it's a piece worth understanding specifically, since state rules differ substantially from federal rules on deductions, credits, and what types of income are taxable. Use this calculator to get an accurate New Jersey-specific estimate, then combine it with your federal calculation to understand your complete tax burden.

Tax planning works best when you run these numbers before year-end rather than after — a few months of adjusted withholding or an additional estimated payment is far easier than an unexpected balance due in April.
