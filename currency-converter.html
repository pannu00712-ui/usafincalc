What This Currency Converter Does

This converter calculates exchange rates between US dollars and major international currencies, shows the converted amount at the current exchange rate, and highlights the spread between the interbank rate and typical consumer rates (credit cards, banks, money exchange kiosks) so you can estimate the true cost of currency conversion for travel, international transfers, or purchases.

Understanding Exchange Rates

The exchange rate you see in the news is the interbank rate — what banks charge each other when trading currencies in large volumes. This rate is never available to retail consumers. The rate you receive adds a markup: typically 1%–3% for credit cards with no foreign transaction fee, 3%–5% for bank accounts, 5%–15% for airport kiosks and currency exchange booths, and 3%–8% for traditional wire transfers.

For International Travel

The most cost-effective approach: use a no-foreign-transaction-fee credit card for all purchases, and if you need cash, use a no-fee debit card (Schwab Bank, Charles Schwab checking reimburses ATM fees globally) to withdraw from local ATMs at the destination. This combination typically costs 1%–2% total versus 8%–15% at airport exchanges or hotels.

For International Money Transfers

Traditional wire transfers through US banks often charge $25–$45 in flat fees plus a 3%–5% exchange rate markup — making small transfers disproportionately expensive. Wise, Revolut, and similar services have reduced this to near-interbank rates with small percentage fees, often saving 2%–5% on the exchanged amount compared to bank wires.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I exchange money before international travel?

Generally no. Exchange rates at your US bank or before departure are rarely better than what you get using a no-fee debit card at a local ATM in your destination country. The exception: some currencies are difficult to obtain outside their home country — in those cases, a small amount pre-exchanged provides peace of mind on arrival.

Do credit cards charge foreign transaction fees?

Many do — typically 2%–3%. But many travel-focused credit cards (Chase Sapphire, Citi Premier, most Capital One cards, Schwab Platinum) waive these fees entirely. If you travel internationally more than once a year, a no-foreign-transaction-fee card is worth having.

How are exchange rates set?

Currency exchange rates are set by global foreign exchange markets, which trade $7+ trillion daily. Rates fluctuate continuously based on interest rate differentials between countries, inflation, economic data, trade flows, and capital movements. No calculator can predict where rates will be next month — the current rate is the market's collective best estimate of fair value right now.

Are some currencies better to exchange in the US vs. at the destination?

For major currencies (EUR, GBP, JPY, CAD, AUD), exchanging at the destination via ATM is almost always better. For less liquid or controlled currencies (some Middle Eastern or African currencies), exchange options abroad may be limited — research your specific destination.

Key Takeaways

The gap between the interbank exchange rate and what most people pay is real money — especially on large transfers or extended travel. Using the right tools (no-fee cards, specialized transfer services) can save 5%–10% on currency conversion versus the worst options. Always compare the total cost of conversion — rate markup plus fees — not just the listed exchange rate.
