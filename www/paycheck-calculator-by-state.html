Understanding Your Paycheck

Your gross salary is the starting point, but your take-home pay—what actually lands in your bank account—can be significantly different depending on where you live. This Paycheck Calculator by State helps you understand exactly how federal, state, and local taxes impact your net income, so you can make informed decisions about where to live and work.

The difference between gross and net pay can be substantial. For example, a $75,000 salary in Texas (no state income tax) yields about $60,000 in take-home pay, while the same salary in California might net only $53,000—a difference of $7,000 per year. That's why comparing paycheck amounts across states is essential for anyone considering a move or evaluating job offers in different locations.

Key Insight: Nine states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. New Hampshire taxes only interest and dividends, not wages.

How This Calculator Works

This calculator uses 2026 federal and state tax rates to estimate your take-home pay. Here's what it calculates:

  1. Federal Income Tax: Uses 2026 IRS tax brackets (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) based on your filing status.
  2. State Income Tax: Uses 2026 state tax rates, including flat-rate states (e.g., Pennsylvania 3.07%) and progressive states (e.g., California 1%-12.3%).
  3. FICA Taxes: Social Security at 6.2% (up to $176,100 wage base) and Medicare at 1.45% (no cap).
  4. Pre-Tax Deductions: Subtracts 401k contributions, health insurance, and other pre-tax deductions before calculating taxes.
  5. Pay Frequency: Converts annual net pay to weekly, biweekly, semi-monthly, or monthly paychecks.

FICA Taxes: Social Security and Medicare

FICA taxes fund Social Security and Medicare. In 2026:

  • Social Security: 6.2% on earnings up to $176,100. Employers match this amount (12.4% total).
  • Medicare: 1.45% on all earnings, with an additional 0.9% for high earners ($200,000+ single, $250,000+ married).

These are often the largest deductions from your paycheck after federal income tax. They're also known as payroll taxes and appear as FICA on your pay stub.

Common Paycheck Deductions

  • Pre-Tax Deductions: 401(k) contributions, health insurance premiums, HSA/FSA contributions, and commuter benefits. These reduce your taxable income.
  • Post-Tax Deductions: Roth 401(k) contributions, union dues, and wage garnishments. These are taken after taxes.
  • Local Taxes: Some cities (e.g., New York City, Philadelphia) impose local income taxes in addition to state taxes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which states have no income tax?
Nine states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. New Hampshire taxes only interest and dividends, not wages.
What is the difference between gross and net pay?
Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions. Net pay (take-home pay) is the amount you receive after taxes and other deductions are withheld. This calculator shows you both so you can see the impact of taxes on your income.
How are federal tax brackets applied?
Federal tax brackets are progressive—you pay different rates on different portions of your income. For example, a single filer earning $75,000 pays 10% on the first $11,600, 12% on the next $35,550, and 22% on the remaining amount. This is called marginal taxation.
What is the Social Security wage base for 2026?
The Social Security wage base for 2026 is $176,100. This is the maximum amount of earnings subject to the 6.2% Social Security tax. Earnings above this amount are not subject to Social Security tax (but Medicare tax still applies).
Can I deduct state income tax on my federal return?
Yes, you can deduct state and local income taxes (SALT) on your federal return, but the deduction is capped at $10,000 per year ($5,000 if married filing separately). This cap applies to the combined total of state income taxes and property taxes.
What is the difference between a pre-tax and post-tax deduction?
Pre-tax deductions (like 401k contributions and health insurance premiums) are taken from your pay before taxes are calculated, reducing your taxable income. Post-tax deductions (like Roth 401k contributions and wage garnishments) are taken after taxes, so they don't reduce your tax liability.
