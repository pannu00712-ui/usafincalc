📊 S&P 500 — Long-Term Reality

The S&P 500 has returned an average of ~10% annually since 1957 (nominal), or about 7% after inflation. But the path is rarely smooth — it dropped 50% in 2000–2002, 57% in 2008–2009, and 34% in early 2020. Every time, it recovered to new highs.

Time in market beats timing the market: Missing just the 10 best trading days in any decade can cut your returns in half. The evidence strongly favors consistent investing over trying to pick entry points.
Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA): Investing a fixed amount monthly removes the pressure of timing. You buy more shares when prices are low, fewer when high — your average cost improves automatically over time.
