What This Calculator Does

This calculator converts between hourly wage and annual salary in both directions — hourly to annual and annual to hourly — using standard work year assumptions (40 hours/week, 52 weeks, 2,080 hours annually). It also shows per-period pay at common pay frequencies and estimates take-home pay after federal and state income tax and FICA.

It's most useful when comparing a salaried offer against current hourly work, evaluating a contractor rate versus employment salary, or understanding what your annual salary actually means in hourly terms.

The Standard Work Year

2,080 hours (52 weeks × 40 hours) is the baseline for hourly-to-salary conversion and is the standard used by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, most employers, and IRS compensation tables. Some calculators use 2,000 hours (50 weeks, accounting for 2 weeks off) — clarify which assumption is being used when comparing numbers across sources.

Salary vs. Hourly: The Hidden Differences

Benefits

Salaried positions typically include employer-paid health insurance, retirement matching, paid time off, and other benefits that aren't reflected in the base salary number. The total compensation value can be $10,000–$20,000+ above the stated salary. Converting a salary offer to hourly without accounting for benefits understates the salaried job's true value.

Overtime

Hourly workers (non-exempt under FLSA) must receive 1.5× their regular rate for hours above 40/week. Exempt salaried employees receive no additional compensation for overtime. For roles where significant overtime is expected, this distinction has a major real-world income impact.

Schedule Flexibility

Many salaried roles involve implicit expectations of work beyond 40 hours. A $70,000 salary for someone regularly working 50 hours/week is actually $33.65/hour — below the stated hourly equivalent for 40-hour work.

Contractor vs. Employee Conversion

Converting an hourly employee wage to a contractor rate requires a markup because contractors pay both sides of FICA (additional 7.65%), fund their own benefits, and have no employer contributions. The standard rule of thumb: multiply your desired employee-equivalent hourly rate by 1.25–1.4 to arrive at an appropriate contractor rate that covers self-employment tax and benefits gap.

Example: You want to match a $40/hour W-2 job as a contractor. Including SE tax and benefits gap: contractor rate of $50–$56/hour is typically needed to achieve equivalent total compensation.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Always compare total compensation, not just hourly or salary — two offers with different pay structures require benefit value, overtime potential, and expected hours to compare accurately.
  • Account for unpaid time off in hourly calculations — if hourly work has 2 weeks of unpaid downtime per year (no paid vacation), effective annual income is based on 2,000 hours, not 2,080.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is $20/hour annually?

$20 × 2,080 hours = $41,600/year gross. After federal income tax and FICA (single, no pre-tax deductions, Texas): approximately $33,000–$34,000 take-home annually, or about $2,750–$2,850/month.

How much do I need to earn hourly to live comfortably?

It depends entirely on location and personal circumstances. MIT's Living Wage Calculator provides location-specific estimates of the wage needed to cover basic expenses. As a rough benchmark, covering rent in most mid-sized US cities without spending more than 30% of income typically requires $18–$25/hour for a single person. Major coastal cities require significantly more.

Is 15 dollars an hour enough to live on?

In most US cities, $15/hour (~$31,200/year gross, ~$25,000–$26,000 net) is challenging as a sole income source. It can cover basic expenses in lower-cost rural areas or with roommates; it's insufficient for most urban markets without housing assistance, a second income, or additional assistance programs.

Key Takeaways

Converting between hourly and salary is simple arithmetic — $X per hour × 2,080 hours = annual salary. The harder comparisons are total compensation comparisons that account for benefits, overtime, expected hours, and for contractors, self-employment tax burden. When evaluating job offers, the take-home pay in your bank account after taxes — not the gross hourly or salary figure — is the number that determines what your life actually costs and what you can save.

How the Hourly to Salary Calculator Works

Enter your hourly wage, how many hours you work per week, and how many weeks per year you work (typically 50–52). The calculator converts this to annual salary, monthly pay, bi-weekly pay, and weekly pay, and shows an estimated after-tax take-home based on standard federal tax brackets.

Formula

Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × Hours per Week × Weeks per Year

Monthly Pay = Annual Salary ÷ 12
Bi-Weekly Pay = Annual Salary ÷ 26
Weekly Pay = Annual Salary ÷ 52

Example: $25/hour × 40 hours × 52 weeks = $52,000 per year

Example

If you earn $22 per hour working 40 hours a week for 52 weeks, your gross annual salary is $45,760. That works out to $3,813 per month before taxes. After federal income tax, Social Security (6.2%), and Medicare (1.45%), your estimated take-home is roughly $35,600 per year — about $2,967 per month for a single filer with no dependents.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert salary to hourly?

Divide your annual salary by 2,080 (40 hours × 52 weeks). A $52,000 salary equals $25 per hour. A rough mental shorthand: divide your annual salary by 2,000 for a close estimate — the small difference accounts for paid time off.

Are hourly workers paid overtime?

Most hourly workers covered by the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) must be paid 1.5× their regular rate for any hours over 40 in a workweek. Some exempt categories (certain agricultural, domestic, and managerial workers) do not qualify. Check your state laws too — some states require overtime after 8 hours in a single day.

Should I include overtime when calculating annual salary?

Use your base hourly rate and regular hours for a reliable baseline. If overtime is consistent, you can add it separately, but do not rely on overtime income for fixed expenses since it can be reduced or eliminated by your employer.

What about tips, bonuses, and commissions?

Tips and commissions are counted as taxable income but not included in this calculator. If your compensation includes these, add them to your calculated annual total for a complete income picture. Note that employers can pay tipped workers a lower base wage (as low as $2.13 federal minimum for tipped workers) if tips bring total compensation to at least the standard minimum wage.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

✗ Not accounting for unpaid time off

If you take 2 weeks of unpaid vacation or face seasonal slowdowns, your actual annual earnings are lower than the 52-week calculation suggests. Use your actual expected paid weeks for a realistic figure.

✗ Ignoring employer benefits when comparing hourly vs salary

A salaried position at $50,000 with health insurance and a 401(k) match may be worth more than an hourly position that pays $26/hour ($54,000) with no benefits. Health insurance alone can be worth $6,000–$15,000 per year.

✗ Forgetting self-employment tax for 1099 workers

If you are a contractor or freelancer paid hourly, you owe self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings up to the Social Security wage base) in addition to income tax. Your effective tax rate is significantly higher than an equivalent W-2 employee.

✗ Comparing gross figures across different states

Take-home pay on the same hourly wage varies significantly by state. Texas and Florida have no state income tax; California tops out at 13.3%. Use our state-specific salary calculators for accurate comparisons.
