What income do you need?

At $2,608/month, you need at least $9,313/month gross income ($111,750/year) under the 28% rule. This income level typically corresponds to senior professionals, dual-income households in the $120k–$160k range, or buyers with significant other assets. Frequently asked questions

What is the monthly payment on a $500,000 mortgage?
With 20% down and a 6.8% 30-year fixed rate, the monthly principal and interest payment is $2,608. Add property tax (varies by state — typically $200–$600/month) and homeowners insurance (~$150/month) for total housing cost.
How much do I need to earn to afford a $500,000 home?
Using the 28% rule, you need at least $111,750/year gross income. In practice, lenders also look at your total debt-to-income ratio. A 36% DTI limit means no more than 36% of gross income going to all debt payments combined.
How much interest will I pay on a $500,000 mortgage?
Over 30 years at 6.8%, total interest paid is approximately $215,509. This is why making extra principal payments early in the loan can save tens of thousands in interest.
Should I put more than 20% down?
Putting 20% down avoids PMI (private mortgage insurance), which adds $50–$200/month. A larger down payment lowers your monthly payment and total interest, but reduces cash on hand. The right amount depends on your financial situation and local market.

What a $500,000 Mortgage Actually Costs Per Month

The purchase price of $500,000 is only the starting point. Your actual monthly housing cost combines principal and interest on the loan, property tax, homeowners insurance, and — if you put less than 20% down — private mortgage insurance (PMI). Understanding all four components before you make an offer prevents the common shock of a mortgage payment significantly larger than expected from the listing price alone.

At a 6.75% interest rate on a 30-year mortgage with 20% down ($100,000 down, $400,000 loan), your estimated monthly principal and interest is $2,594. Adding typical property tax and homeowners insurance brings total PITI to approximately $3,344/month — though property tax varies dramatically by state and county.

Income Required for a $500,000 Home

Using the standard lender guideline that PITI should not exceed 28%–36% of gross monthly income, a $500,000 home with 20% down requires approximately $11,943–$10,450/year in gross income to qualify comfortably. With 10% down and PMI, that income requirement rises to $14,114–$12,350/year.

Lenders also consider total debt-to-income ratio (all debts, not just mortgage) — typically capped at 43%–45%. If you carry significant student loans, auto payments, or credit card minimums, those reduce the mortgage payment a lender will approve you for at any given income level.

Total Interest Over 30 Years

On a $400,000 loan at 6.75%, you pay approximately $533,981 in interest over 30 years — meaning the total cost of the home (down payment + all loan payments) is roughly $1,033,981. This figure demonstrates why prepaying principal, even modestly, produces substantial long-run savings: an extra $500/month applied to principal would cut payoff time by 4–5 years and save tens of thousands in interest.

15-Year vs. 30-Year Mortgage

A 15-year mortgage on a $500,000 home (20% down) at typically 6.0%–6.25% carries a monthly P&I of approximately $3,485 — significantly higher than the 30-year payment of $2,594. But the total interest paid drops from ~$533,981 to ~$227,213 — a difference of $306,768. Whether the higher monthly payment is manageable determines which term is appropriate for your financial situation.

Closing Costs on a $500,000 Home

Expect closing costs of 2%–4% of purchase price: $10,000–$20,000, in addition to your down payment. This means your total cash needed at closing is your down payment plus closing costs — for a 20% down scenario, $110,000–$120,000 total. Budget for this well in advance of making an offer.

Common Mistakes

Qualifying for maximum vs. buying at maximum

A lender may approve you for a $500,000 purchase based on debt-to-income ratios. That doesn't mean a $500,000 home fits your actual monthly budget. Lenders calculate against gross income; you live on net income. Model the PITI against your actual take-home pay to verify the payment is comfortable, not just technically approvable.

Ignoring property tax variation

The same $500,000 home in New Jersey (effective rate ~2.2%) costs $440/month more in property tax than in Hawaii (effective rate ~0.28%). Research the specific tax rate for your target county before assuming the estimates above apply to your situation.

Tips and Best Practices

  • Get multiple loan quotes — rate differences of 0.25%–0.5% on a $500,000 loan can translate to $40–$80/month and $15,000–$30,000 over 30 years.
  • Model rate scenarios — at 7.5% instead of 6.75%, your monthly P&I on this home increases by approximately $202/month. Know your payment at different rate levels before committing.
  • Build in a maintenance budget — beyond PITI, plan for 1%–1.5% of home value annually in maintenance. On a $500,000 home: $5,000–$7,500/year, or $417–$625/month set aside for repairs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What income do I need to afford a $500,000 home?

At $3,344/month PITI (20% down, 6.75% rate), you need approximately $143,314–$125,400/year gross income using the 28%–32% PITI-to-income guideline. This assumes no other significant debt; existing student loans or car payments reduce the mortgage income requirement.

How much do I need saved before buying a $500,000 home?

Minimum: 3.5% FHA down payment ($17,500) plus 2%–4% closing costs ($10,000–$20,000), plus 3–6 months of reserves post-closing. A realistic minimum cash reserve is $42,532–$52,564 including closing costs. With 20% down: $125,032–$135,064.

Is $500,000 a lot for a house?

It depends on the market. $500,000 is above the US median home price, typical for coastal markets, major metros, or larger/newer construction in mid-tier markets. In San Francisco, $500,000 buys a studio; in many Midwest cities, it buys a 4-bedroom suburban home. Local market context is the only meaningful reference point.

What credit score do I need for a $500,000 mortgage?

620 minimum for most conventional loans; 580 for FHA loans (with 3.5% down). But getting a competitive rate — the difference between 6.5% and 7.25% on this loan amount is $134/month — typically requires a 720+ score, and the best rates are reserved for 760+.

How long does it take to pay off a $500,000 mortgage?

30 years on a standard mortgage, 15 on a 15-year term. Making one extra monthly payment per year reduces a 30-year mortgage to roughly 26 years and saves several years of interest. Biweekly payments (half-payment every 2 weeks = 26 half-payments, or 13 full payments/year) produce a similar effect automatically.

Key Takeaways

A $500,000 home purchase requires planning well beyond the purchase price: cash for down payment plus closing costs, income sufficient for the full PITI payment (not just principal and interest), and an ongoing maintenance reserve. The monthly payment at a specific rate and down payment scenario is deterministic — use this calculator and the mortgage calculator to model your exact scenario. Then verify that the resulting payment fits comfortably in your actual monthly budget after taxes, existing debts, and savings goals, not just against lender DTI limits.
