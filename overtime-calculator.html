Frequently Asked Questions

How is overtime pay calculated?
Under federal law, overtime pay is 1.5 times your regular hourly rate for any hours worked beyond 40 in a single workweek. For example, a $20/hour employee earns $30/hour for overtime hours.
Is overtime based on daily or weekly hours?
Federal law (the Fair Labor Standards Act) calculates overtime based on a 40-hour workweek, not a daily threshold. Some states, such as California, have additional daily overtime rules that can require overtime pay after 8 hours in a single day, regardless of weekly total.
Does everyone qualify for overtime pay?
No. Overtime rules generally apply to non-exempt hourly employees. Many salaried employees in executive, administrative, or professional roles above a certain salary threshold are classified as exempt and do not receive overtime pay.
What is double-time pay?
Double-time (2x your regular rate) is not required by federal law but is sometimes required by state law (such as California after 12 hours in a day) or offered voluntarily by employers for holidays or extreme overtime hours.

What This Overtime Pay Calculator Does

This calculator computes your overtime pay, total weekly earnings, and effective hourly rate when you work beyond your standard hours. It handles federal overtime rules under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), time-and-a-half calculations, double-time provisions common in some states and industries, and the after-tax take-home estimate for overtime hours.

How It Works

Overtime Rate = Regular Hourly Rate × 1.5

Overtime Pay = Overtime Hours × Overtime Rate

Total Weekly Pay = (Regular Hours × Regular Rate) + Overtime Pay

For salaried non-exempt employees, the regular rate is calculated by dividing total compensation for the week by total hours worked (including overtime hours), then multiplying overtime hours by 0.5× that rate — since the salary already covers straight-time for all hours.

Key Concepts Explained

FLSA Overtime Eligibility

The FLSA requires overtime (1.5× rate) for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek for non-exempt employees. As of 2024, salaried workers earning below $684/week ($35,568/year) are generally non-exempt and eligible for overtime regardless of job title. Workers classified as "executive, administrative, professional" (EAP) exemptions must earn above the threshold AND primarily perform exempt duties.

Daily vs. Weekly Overtime

Federal law calculates overtime on a weekly basis (40+ hours in a workweek). However, California requires overtime for hours over 8 in a day and double time for hours over 12. Alaska and Nevada also have daily overtime provisions. Check your state's rules, which may be more protective than federal minimums.

Tax Withholding on Overtime

Overtime pay is taxed at the same rates as regular wages — there is no separate "overtime tax rate." However, because overtime increases your total paycheck, your employer may withhold at a higher effective rate. Both withholding methods result in the same annual tax liability; only the timing differs.

Tips & Best Practices

  • Track your hours carefully — non-exempt employees should maintain their own records. Your legal right to overtime pay cannot be waived by you or your employer.
  • Report all overtime on your time card — working "off the clock" at employer pressure is an FLSA violation. Report all hours worked.
  • Understand your exemption classification — being called a "manager" or paid a salary does not automatically make you exempt. If you believe you've been misclassified, consult your state's Department of Labor or an employment attorney.
  • Model after-tax take-home before committing to heavy OT — 20 hours of overtime at 1.5× may net only 12–14 straight-time hours worth of take-home pay after taxes.
  • Understand comp time rules — private sector employers generally cannot legally substitute comp time for overtime cash pay under the FLSA. Public sector employers may offer comp time.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does overtime kick in after 8 hours a day or 40 hours a week?

Under federal law (FLSA), overtime is based on 40 hours per workweek — not per day. California requires overtime for hours over 8 in a single day, and double time for hours over 12. Always check your state's overtime rules, which may be more protective than federal minimums.

Is overtime taxed at a higher rate?

No — overtime wages are taxed at the same marginal rates as regular wages. However, because they increase total compensation, they may be taxed at a higher marginal rate if they push your income into the next bracket. Your total annual tax liability is the same regardless of how wages are spread throughout the year.

Can my employer make me work overtime?

Yes — in most US at-will employment states, employers can require overtime and can terminate employees who refuse (absent a union contract or employment agreement). Mandatory overtime is legal in most states, with exceptions for regulated industries like trucking (hours-of-service rules) and medical resident programs.

What is double time and when does it apply?

Double time means 2× your regular rate. California requires double time for hours over 12 in a day and for hours beyond 8 on the seventh consecutive day of work. Some collective bargaining agreements include double time for holidays or specific shifts. Federal law does not require double time for any scenario.
