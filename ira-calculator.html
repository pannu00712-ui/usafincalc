What This IRA Calculator Does

This IRA calculator projects the future value of your Individual Retirement Account based on current balance, annual contributions, investment return assumptions, and years to retirement. It models both Traditional and Roth IRAs — showing after-tax balances at retirement so you can make a true comparison between the two account types based on your current and expected future tax rates.

How It Works

IRA growth follows compound growth applied to annual contributions:

FV = Current Balance × (1+r)ⁿ + Annual Contribution × [((1+r)ⁿ − 1) / r] × (1+r)

For Traditional IRAs, the after-tax withdrawal value applies your expected retirement tax rate to the full balance. For Roth IRAs, the full projected balance is available tax-free in retirement.

Key Concepts Explained

2024 IRA Contribution Limits

The 2024 limit is $7,000 per person ($8,000 if age 50 or older). This limit applies to the combined total of all your Traditional and Roth IRAs — you can split contributions between accounts but cannot exceed the total limit per person.

Traditional vs. Roth IRA Tax Treatment

Traditional IRA contributions may be tax-deductible (reducing taxable income today), growing tax-deferred. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Roth contributions are after-tax (no current deduction), but all qualified withdrawals in retirement — including all growth — are completely tax-free. If you expect a higher tax bracket in retirement, Roth typically wins.

Roth IRA Income Limits

For 2024, Roth contributions phase out between $146,000–$161,000 MAGI for single filers and $230,000–$240,000 for married filing jointly. Above those thresholds, the "backdoor Roth" — a non-deductible Traditional IRA contribution followed by a Roth conversion — is available at any income level.

Tips & Best Practices

  • Contribute early in the year — an IRA contribution made January 1 earns a full year of returns vs. one made April 15. Over decades, early-year contributions meaningfully increase ending balances.
  • Use the backdoor Roth if income limits apply — high earners can contribute to a non-deductible Traditional IRA and immediately convert to Roth. Be aware of the pro-rata rule if you have other pre-tax IRA balances.
  • Invest your IRA contributions, don't just deposit them — many people contribute but leave funds in a money market account. The IRA is a tax wrapper; you must invest the funds inside it to benefit from tax-advantaged growth.
  • Name beneficiaries carefully — IRAs pass outside your will via beneficiary designation. Keep designations current. Non-spouse beneficiaries must generally deplete inherited IRAs within 10 years under SECURE 2.0 rules.
  • Consider a Roth conversion ladder for early retirees — converting Traditional IRA funds to Roth each year in retirement in lower-bracket amounts builds a 5-year ladder for penalty-free early access.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I contribute to both a 401(k) and an IRA in the same year?

Yes. IRA contributions are independent of 401(k) contributions. You can max out both ($23,000 in 401k + $7,000 in IRA = $30,000 in 2024). However, if you're covered by a workplace retirement plan, your Traditional IRA contribution may not be deductible depending on your MAGI.

What happens if I contribute too much to my IRA?

Excess IRA contributions are subject to a 6% excise tax per year the excess remains in the account. Withdraw the excess plus earnings before your tax filing deadline to avoid the penalty. If discovered after the deadline, apply it to the next year's limit or withdraw it and pay the 6% penalty.

At what age can I withdraw penalty-free?

Penalty-free distributions from Traditional IRAs start at age 59½. Before that, a 10% early withdrawal penalty applies plus ordinary income tax on the full distribution. Several exceptions exist: first-time home purchase (up to $10,000 lifetime), higher education expenses, disability, and substantially equal periodic payments (SEPP/72t).

What is a spousal IRA?

A spousal IRA allows a non-working or lower-earning spouse to contribute based on the working spouse's earned income. Both spouses can contribute up to $7,000 each (2024) as long as combined earned income equals or exceeds combined contributions — doubling the couple's annual tax-advantaged savings capacity.
