What This Discount Calculator Does

This calculator quickly computes the sale price, dollar savings, and effective discount percentage for any item or purchase. Whether you're comparing sale prices, calculating the value of a coupon or promo code, figuring out bulk discount savings, or working out markdowns on your own products, this tool does the math instantly without manual percentage calculations.

You can use it three ways: enter original price and discount percentage to find sale price; enter original price and sale price to find percentage off; or work backwards from a desired price to find the required discount percentage. It also handles stacked discounts — which are different from simply adding the percentages.

How It Works

The core discount formulas are:

Sale Price = Original Price × (1 − Discount % / 100)

Savings Amount = Original Price − Sale Price

Discount % = (Savings Amount / Original Price) × 100

For stacked discounts (e.g., 20% off, then additional 10% off), the combined discount is NOT simply 30%. Correct calculation: 20% off leaves 80%, then 10% off that leaves 72% of original. Combined savings is 28%, not 30%.

Key Concepts Explained

Percentage Off vs. Dollar Off

Percentage discounts scale with purchase price; fixed-dollar discounts do not. A 20% discount on a $50 item saves $10. "$10 off" saves the same on a $50 item but only $10 on a $200 item (vs $40 for 20% off). Always convert to the same unit for the specific item you're buying.

BOGO Discounts

"Buy One Get One Free" equals a 50% discount on each item if you need both. "Buy One Get One 50% Off" equals a 25% overall discount. These are less favorable than they appear — only if you genuinely need both items does the offer represent real savings.

Markdown vs. Margin

For retailers, a markdown reduces the retail price by a percentage of that price. Margin is profit as a percentage of the selling price. If an item costs $40 and retails for $100 (60% margin), discounting it 30% to $70 reduces margin to approximately 43%.

Tips & Best Practices

  • Check price history before assuming it's a deal — some retailers inflate regular prices to make discounts appear larger. Use tools like CamelCamelCamel for Amazon or Google Shopping for price comparisons.
  • Stack coupons strategically — manufacturer coupons can often be combined with store sales and cashback apps (Ibotta, Rakuten). Each layer applies to the already-reduced price.
  • Know the return policy for discounted items — some sale and clearance items are non-returnable. Factor this risk into the "deal" calculation.
  • For business pricing, use gross margin not markup — markup is calculated on cost; margin is on selling price. A 50% markup gives only a 33% margin.
  • Calculate cost-per-unit for bulk discounts — divide the discounted total by units to compare per-unit costs across different quantity tiers.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate 30% off a price?

Multiply the original price by 0.70 (which is 1 minus 0.30). For example, 30% off $85 = $85 × 0.70 = $59.50. Or find 30% of the price ($85 × 0.30 = $25.50) and subtract from the original ($85 − $25.50 = $59.50).

If I take 20% off and then 10% off, is that 30% total?

No. Stacked percentage discounts multiply rather than add. 20% off leaves 80%. An additional 10% off that reduces it to 72% of the original (0.80 × 0.90 = 0.72). Total discount is 28%, not 30%.

How do I find the original price from a discounted price?

Divide the sale price by (1 − discount percentage). If an item is $63 after a 30% discount: $63 ÷ 0.70 = $90 original price. This formula is essential for reverse-engineering sale prices.

Is sales tax applied to the original or discounted price?

In most US states, sales tax is applied to the final sale price after discounts. If you buy a $100 item at 20% off for $80 in a state with 8% sales tax, you pay tax on $80 ($6.40), not $100 ($8.00). Manufacturer coupons may be handled differently in some states.
